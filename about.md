<h1>About</h1>

<div class="about-section">
<p>I've spent over 20 years helping people learn how to do their jobs better: how to sell a product, run a new process, or handle a system that just changed on them.</p>

<p>I started in 2000 as a retail sales consultant at Cingular, picked up Acting Manager duties along the way, then moved into B2B outside sales. From there I moved into training, coaching store teams in the field and later training other trainers, helping hundreds of new hires a year learn their new job and thousands of existing employees learn how to sell the newest products and services. As Cingular became AT&T, I spent close to a decade as an instructional designer building classroom and eLearning courses at scale, then moved into product marketing management, running content strategy for AT&T's device support site.</p>

<p>In 2019 I moved into tech, first at Facebook building enablement for the global ad sales org, then at LinkedIn, where I eventually led a team of designers and developers building learning experiences that reached every employee at the company. I've also done consulting work on the side since 2021.</p>
</div>

<h2>How I think about L&amp;D</h2>

<div class="about-section">
<p>I try to find the real problem before I pick a format. On one leadership coaching program, most people were dropping out before finishing, even though the course reviewed well. The easy read was "managers are too busy." The data said something else, so we rebuilt around that instead.</p>

<p>I've also learned the newer, flashier tool isn't automatically the better one. We piloted VR-based training once and it lost to something much simpler: people practicing with a peer, or a manager riding along on a real client call. Simple beats flashy more often than you'd think.</p>

<p>I lead teams now, but I still like building things myself. And credit matters to me. The work I'm proudest of was built by teams, not by me alone.</p>
</div>
